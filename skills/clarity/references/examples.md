# Clarity — Before/After Examples

---

## 1. Technical — Release Note

**Before**

> It is important to note that in this release we have made a number of improvements to the way in which the system handles authentication. There has been an implementation of a new token refresh mechanism that may potentially result in reduced session timeouts for users who are currently experiencing this issue.

**After**

> This release fixes session timeouts. A new token-refresh mechanism keeps sessions alive without requiring users to log back in.

**What changed:** The warm-up ("It is important to note that") and the hedge ("may potentially result in") were cut. "Made a number of improvements to the way in which" — a nominalization chain — became a single verb phrase. "Users who are currently experiencing this issue" was replaced with a concrete benefit statement.

---

## 2. Professional — Meeting Note

**Before**

> There was a discussion had by the team regarding the matter of timeline slippage. It was felt by several attendees that there may be some risk associated with the current approach, and it was suggested that perhaps a re-evaluation of priorities might be something worth considering going forward.

**After**

> The team discussed timeline slippage. Three people flagged the current approach as risky and asked to reprioritize.

**What changed:** Passive constructions ("was had by", "was felt by", "was suggested") were converted to active voice with known actors restored. Hedge stack ("may be some risk… perhaps… might be… worth considering") collapsed into a direct statement. "Going forward" — a classic warm-up trailer — was cut entirely.

---

## 3. Persuasive — Pitch Opening

**Before**

> I would like to take this opportunity to share with you some thoughts on what I believe could potentially represent a significant improvement in the area of how our team currently approaches the onboarding process for new hires.

**After**

> New-hire onboarding takes six weeks. It shouldn't. Here's how to cut it to two.

**What changed:** The entire opening sentence deferred the point by 30 words. The rewrite leads with the problem (six weeks), challenges it immediately, then previews the solution. All nominalizations ("improvement in the area of how we currently approach") were replaced with a concrete claim (cut it to two weeks).
