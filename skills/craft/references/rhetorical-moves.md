# Rhetorical Moves Reference

Six moves used in `prose-shaper:craft`. Load this file when you need more detail than the SKILL.md table provides.

---

## 1. One True Sentence

**Definition:** Replace a vague or inflated claim with a single sentence that is specific enough to be verifiable and too precise to dismiss.

**When to use:** Whenever the prose makes a claim that could apply to any product, project, or argument. Look for words like "significant," "much better," "really improved," or "major impact."

**When NOT to use:** When the specific number or fact is unavailable, contested, or would require a footnote that undermines momentum.

**Example:**
- Wrong: The migration improved performance significantly.
- Good: Page load dropped from 4.2 seconds to 0.9 seconds after the migration.

---

## 2. Surprise Detail

**Definition:** A counterintuitive, unexpected, or strikingly specific observation that breaks the reader's autopilot and forces attention.

**When to use:** In openings, in the middle of a long argument to re-engage attention, or wherever the prose risks becoming predictable.

**When NOT to use:** When the detail is merely quirky rather than illuminating — surprise for its own sake is noise.

**Example:**
- Wrong: The launch event had some unusual moments.
- Good: The VP closed her laptop three minutes into the demo. She opened it again to take notes.

---

## 3. Antithesis

**Definition:** A tight parallel construction that sets two contrasting ideas against each other in the same sentence or adjacent sentences.

**When to use:** When you need to crystallize a choice, raise stakes, or define something by what it is not.

**When NOT to use:** When the contrast is false or oversimplified. Antithesis that papers over nuance damages trust.

**Example:**
- Wrong: The old system had problems, but the new one is better in most ways.
- Good: Not a rewrite. A replacement.

---

## 4. Prolepsis

**Definition:** Anticipating and naming the reader's most likely objection before they raise it, then reframing or absorbing it.

**When to use:** Before asking the reader to accept something uncomfortable — a risk, a cost, a counterintuitive conclusion.

**When NOT to use:** When the objection is minor or the audience is already aligned. Raising objections that don't exist creates doubt.

**Example:**
- Wrong: Despite some concerns, this is the right path forward.
- Good: You'll say the timeline is aggressive. It is. But the alternative — another six-month delay — costs more than the risk.

---

## 5. Repeat with Intention

**Definition:** Deliberate repetition of a word or phrase for rhythm or emphasis — distinct from accidental repetition, which is a clarity error.

**When to use:** At moments of emphasis or resolution. Works best in threes or in call-and-response structures.

**When NOT to use:** More than once or twice per document. Overuse turns emphasis into noise.

**Example:**
- Wrong: They released quickly without caring about quality.
- Good: Ship fast, break things, apologize never.

---

## 6. Rewrite for the Reader

**Definition:** Restructuring a passage so the reader's question ("why does this matter to me?") is answered before the writer's exposition begins.

**When to use:** Whenever the prose leads with background, context, or process before landing the point that the reader actually cares about.

**When NOT to use:** When the audience genuinely needs the context first — some technical readers do.

**Example:**
- Wrong: After evaluating six vendors over three months, the team concluded that Option B was the best fit.
- Good: Option B wins. Here's the three-month case for it.
