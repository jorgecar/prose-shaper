# Complication First

The problem this solves: when the situation is familiar to the reader, opening with it tells them nothing new — they tune out. Opening with the complication tells them something is wrong that wasn't wrong before, and the situation becomes useful as the context for an already-felt problem.

**Pattern shape.**

- **Complication** — the rupture, in concrete and specific terms.
- **Situation** — the state of affairs the complication interrupted, just enough to make the rupture legible.
- **Question** — usually implicit; the complication forces it.
- **Answer** — your point.

**Example.**

- Wrong (Standard SCQA, dry opener): The customer-data export job runs every Sunday and has done so since 2021. Recently, the job has been failing intermittently. We need to fix it.
- Good (Complication First): Three weeks of customer-data exports are missing. The Sunday job that produces them has run unchanged since 2021, but in late October it began failing intermittently — and the alerts went to a dashboard nobody owns. We should move the job to the new pipeline and route its alerts to the on-call rotation.

The Good version makes the reader feel the cost in the first sentence (*three weeks missing*). The situation arrives as context for an already-real problem, not as a setup for a hypothetical one.

**When it works.** Urgent escalations, post-incident reports, op-eds, any document where the reader must care by the end of the first sentence or you will lose them.

**When it backfires.** The audience is the party responsible for the complication — leading with their failure puts them in defense mode before they read your argument. Routine status updates where every week has a complication, and leading with one makes the document indistinguishable from noise. Audiences who need warming up; complication-first lands cold.
