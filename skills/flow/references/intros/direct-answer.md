# Direct Answer

The problem this solves: executive readers stop reading after the first paragraph in roughly the order they stopped caring. If the answer doesn't appear there, they decide based on the situation alone, or they skip to the recommendation and miss the framing entirely.

**Pattern shape.**

- **Answer** — your point, in the first sentence or two.
- **Situation** — the state of affairs the recommendation applies to (briefer than in Standard SCQA — the reader is presumed to know it).
- **Complication** — what changed to force the question.
- **Question** — usually implicit, sometimes named in passing.

**Example.**

- Wrong (Standard SCQA for an executive): Our deploy pipeline has run unchanged since 2022, when we last invested in CI infrastructure. Over the last six months, build times have climbed from twelve minutes to forty-one. The on-call team has absorbed three pages this month from deploys timing out. We should rebuild the pipeline.
- Good (Direct Answer): Rebuild the deploy pipeline before the end of the quarter. Build times have climbed from twelve minutes to forty-one since the last CI investment in 2022, and three pages this month came from deploy timeouts alone. The cost of waiting is on-call attrition; the cost of acting is one engineer for six weeks.

The Good version leads with the recommendation. An executive who reads only the first sentence gets the decision; the rest is the case for it.

**When it works.** Executive memos, board updates, short proposals to decision-makers who already lived through the situation, any document where the recommendation is what the reader is hunting for.

**When it backfires.** Audiences who don't yet share your framing — they will reject the answer because they would have asked a different question. Educational or expository documents where the reader needs the context to understand the answer. Reports to skeptical readers who need to see your reasoning before they accept your conclusion.
