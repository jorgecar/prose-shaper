# Consultative

The problem this solves: in long documents, readers rarely read top to bottom. They jump to the section that names their concern, read it, and leave. Without a mini-introduction, each section assumes the reader read the previous one — and the section's point lands as a non sequitur to anyone who dipped in.

**Pattern shape.**

- The document opens with a Standard SCQA (or another top-level pattern). Then each major section also opens with a compressed S/C/Q/A — usually one sentence each, sometimes folded into a single paragraph — before stating the section's point.

**Example.**

- Wrong (no mini-intro, second section of a long report): **Pricing model.** We recommend moving to per-seat pricing with a usage-based add-on for storage above 500GB. The shift would simplify quoting, align with how competitors price, and unlock the upmarket segment we've struggled to win.
- Good (consultative mini-intro): **Pricing model.** Today we price per workspace, with no relation to seats or storage — a structure inherited from the early years when most customers were teams of three to five. The customer mix has shifted: the median deal is now twenty-two seats, and half of new opportunities request a quote we cannot generate without a manual exception. The question this raises — how should we price for the customer base we now serve? — is the subject of this section. We recommend moving to per-seat pricing with a usage-based add-on for storage above 500GB.

The Good version reorients a reader who landed in the pricing section without reading the overview. Three sentences of mini-S/C/Q earn the recommendation that follows.

**When it works.** Long reports (ten pages or more), multi-section proposals, technical deep-dives with stand-alone sections, documents the reader will reference repeatedly rather than read once.

**When it backfires.** Short documents — mini-intros become throat-clearing the reader has to skip past. Sections that follow obviously from one another, where a mini-intro repeats the previous section's conclusion. Action-oriented documents (status updates, runbooks) where the reader wants the action, not the setup.
